# Multimodal

> Vision encoders, audio, video, late vs early fusion. Combining language with sensory modalities in one model.

## What "multimodal" means in 2026

Modern frontier LLMs accept and produce a mix of:

- **Text** — the default.
- **Images** — input universally (GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet, Gemini Pro, Llama 3.2 V, Qwen-VL). Output for image-generation models.
- **Audio** — input speech recognition + understanding; output text-to-speech. Native audio modes in GPT-4o, Gemini.
- **Video** — input (Gemini, Qwen2-VL). Output for diffusion video models.

This chapter focuses on the *language-model* side: how you feed non-text modalities into a transformer.

## Vision: the canonical multimodal addition

The dominant pattern: **vision encoder + projector + LLM**.

```
image → ViT encoder → vision tokens (N × D)
                                       │
                                       ▼
                            [projector: linear or MLP]
                                       │
                                       ▼
                    text tokens + vision tokens → LLM
```

The vision encoder is typically a CLIP-style ViT [Radford et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2103.00020)[^clip] producing 256–1024 patch tokens per image. The projector maps these into the LLM's hidden dimension.

LLaVA [Liu et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2304.08485)[^llava] established this pattern at modest scale; it's the basis for most open multimodal LLMs.

## Late vs early fusion

- **Late fusion** (LLaVA-style) — frozen pre-trained vision encoder; project; concat with text; let the LLM attend over both. Cheap; works well.
- **Early fusion** — image tokens enter early; modality types share most of the network. Examples: Chameleon [Meta, 2024](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2405.09818)[^chameleon], native multimodal Gemini.

Early fusion has higher capability ceiling but is much harder to train. The community split: open models mostly do late fusion; frontier closed models lean early.

## Tokenisation of images

Two approaches:

1. **Continuous embeddings** — each patch becomes a hidden-dim vector; concatenated with text token embeddings. No discrete tokenizer. LLaVA pattern.
2. **Discrete image tokens** — quantize image patches into a vocabulary of "image tokens" (VQ-VAE, Lookup-Free Quantization). Treat as text tokens. Used by Chameleon, Lumina, some Gemini variants.

Discrete tokens enable joint generation (text + image out of one auto-regressive head); continuous embeddings can't generate images.

## Resolution and tile pyramids

A single fixed-resolution ViT can't handle a high-res image well. Solutions:

- **Tile pyramids** [Liu et al., 2024 (LLaVA-NeXT)](https://github.com/LLaVA-VL/LLaVA-NeXT) — tile the image at multiple resolutions; encode each tile; pass all tokens.
- **Dynamic resolution** [Qwen2-VL](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2409.12191)[^qwen2vl] — accept arbitrary resolutions natively.
- **Compression** — use a pooling layer or perceiver-style resampler to reduce token count.

For document / OCR / detailed-image tasks, resolution is everything.

## Audio

Two regimes:

1. **ASR + text** — speech is transcribed (Whisper-style); the LLM sees text. Lossy on tone and prosody.
2. **Native audio** — audio is tokenised (SoundStream, EnCodec) into discrete codes; the LLM is trained on text + audio jointly. GPT-4o "advanced voice" works this way.

Native audio enables much better naturalness in voice agents (no pause, can interrupt) but requires a much more involved training pipeline.

## Video

Video = many image frames. Naïve approach (one frame per second, treat each as an image) works for short videos but blows up the token budget.

Frontier video understanding (Gemini, Qwen2-VL, Video-LLaVA) uses:

- Spatiotemporal patches (3D patches over time + space).
- Frame-rate adaptive sampling (more frames for fast action; fewer for static).
- Long-context (1M+ tokens) to fit a few minutes of video.

Video remains expensive per query; lots of room to improve.

## Training data

Multimodal training data:

- **Image-text pairs** — CLIP-style; web-scraped image captions. Hundreds of millions to billions.
- **Image-instruction data** — synthetic Q&A about images (LLaVA-Instruct). Generated by GPT-4-V over images; widely used.
- **Document data** — PDFs, slides, screenshots with extracted text. Critical for OCR-style tasks.
- **Video-text** — much harder; less of it; recently improving via synthetic captioning.

Quality and dedup matter as much as in pretraining text. See [Pretraining data](../fundamentals/data/pretraining-data.md).

Multimodal benchmarks are particularly contamination-prone — image hashes are easier to detect than text but it still happens.

## Engineering for multimodal serving

- **Token budgets** are different — a single image at high resolution can be 2000+ tokens.
- **Caching** is harder — small image edits produce different tokens.
- **Cost** — image inputs are usually billed at substantial token rates ($1–10 per high-res image at frontier).
- **Latency** — vision encoding adds ~100–500 ms TTFT for non-trivial images.
- **PII** — images can contain faces, license plates, addresses. See [Privacy](../safety/privacy.md).

## A pragmatic multimodal product checklist

When building a multimodal feature:

- [ ] Picked a model that benchmarks well on *your* modality (don't assume "supports images" means "good at document OCR").
- [ ] Measured cost per image at typical resolution.
- [ ] Measured latency under realistic image sizes.
- [ ] Handled the privacy implications of accepting images.
- [ ] Eval set includes failure modes specific to your domain (low light, occluded, multilingual signage, etc.).
- [ ] Considered fallback: if vision fails, can the text alone answer?
